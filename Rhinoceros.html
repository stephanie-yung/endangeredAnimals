Rhinoceros

Extinction Type: Critically Endangered

There are only five species of Rhinoceros out there: Black, Javan, Sumatran, White, and the Greater One-Horned Rhinos. Out of these five species that exist today, the black, Javan, and the Sumatran rhino are listed as critically endangered.

According to the Iternational Union For Conservation of Nature (IUCN) Red list, it mentions there are only 3,142 black rhinos left (as of 2020) and that there is a slow ise in population numbers. Once dominating their species in their vast numbers, they were hunted by poachers for their horns and for their land. Between 1960 and 1995, poaching caused a disastrous 98% reduction in the population.

The Javan and Sumatran rhino, face a dire outlook, with only 18 and 30 mature individuals left respectively. Javan rhinos have been listed as endangered since 1986 and critically endangered since 1996. There are an estimated 68 Javan rhinos living in Ujung Kulon National Park on the western tip of Java, but only 33% of them have the ability to reproduce.

The total population of Sumatran rhinos is estimated to be less than 80, declining by more than 80% over the last 30 years. There are nine of these animals in captivity, eight in Indonesia and one in Malaysia (a non-reproductive female).

How You Can Help:

  • Use Sustainable Wood, Paper, and Palm Oil:

    • Purchasing Certified Sustainable Palm Oil and FSC-Certified Forest Products helps curb demand for illegal logging and forest conversion, meaning the Sumatran and Javan rhino habitats have a better chance of being protected.

  • Volunteer:

    • Some National Park and Wildlife Reserves offer volunteering programs, where individuals can apply to join a ranger team to help monitor wildlife and map sightings.

  • Sponsor A Rhino:

    • For only $55 , one can adopt a Sumatran rhino from the WWF. There are other organizations like: Sheldrick Wildlife Trust, International Rhino Foundation, and Save the Rhino International that all offer adoption programs.
