Sunda Tiger

Sunda tigers are distinguished by heavy black stripes on their orange coats. The last of the Sunda island tigers—estimated to be fewer than 400 today—are holding on for survival in the remaining patches of forest on the island of Sumatra. Accelerating deforestation and rampant poaching mean this noble creature could end up extinct like its Javan and Balinese counterparts.

One of the main threats to Sumatran tigers is poaching. Hunters trap or shoot them for their skin, bones and canines, which are in high demand as status symbols, primarily overseas, and for use in East Asian traditional medicine. Habitat loss due to expansion of oil palm, coffee and acacia plantations, and smallholder encroachment also threaten these big cats.

Dozens, if not hundreds, of conservation groups are working to prevent extinction. Additionally, Sumatran tigers are listed on several endangered and protected species lists, including:

  • The Convention of International Trade in Endangered Species of Wild Fauna and Flora (CITES)
  • Act of the Republic of Indonesia No. 5 Concerning Conservation of Living Resources and their Ecosystems
  • International Union for the Conservation of Nature Red List
