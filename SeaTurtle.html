Sea Turtles

Extinction Risk: Endangered

The earliest marine turtles were known to roam earth about 120 million years ago. Today, there are seven species of sea turtles, in which all are threatened and endangered. Sea turtles use earth’s magnetic fields to navigate the marine environment. They have magnetite in their brains which help orient them to earth’s magnetic pulls. A sea turtle can lay more than 150 eggs at one particular time. Unfortunately, fewer than .1% of hatchlings survive to adulthood. Other animals such as birds and crabs prey on turtles when they first are born. Newborn sea turtles have a higher chance of survival when migrating to the waters in a large pack.

Sea turtles are an extremely endangered species and are constantly threatened. A big threat is fishing. Sea turtles get stuck inside of fishing nets, which prevent their mobility to get oxygen at the surface. Every year, about 4,600 sea turtles are killed by fishing nets or hooks in the United States waters. In addition to the threat of fishing nets, sea turtles are being illegally harvested for their eggs, skins, meat, and shells. An infamous product that is endangering sea turtles are turtle-skin belts. Moreover, there is an abundant amount of pollution within oceans. There are pollutants such as chemicals and toxic metals, that greatly harm marine life. Researchers believe ocean pollution is a primary cause for fibropapillomas. Fibropapillomas is a disease in sea turtles which causes tumors on both external areas and internal organs.

Many organizations such as The Sea Turtle Conservancy and Seeturtles are actively trying to educate individuals, help save sea turtles, and prevent them from going extinct. Furthermore, there are laws enacted such as the Endangered Species Act, which poses legal protection in the United States for sea turtles.

How you can help

  • Join conservation groups to save hatchlings
  • Minimize climate change impacts
  • Reduce the use of plastic items
  • Avoid sunscreens with “oxybenzone”, and use brands with “Reef Friendly” labels.
